History

The earliest Austronesian settlers of the Philippines established a variety of barangay communities under the leadership of local chiefs known as datus, beginning thousands of years ago.

The islands traded with China, Japan, India, and Islamic kingdoms in Southeast Asia prior to European contact.

After arriving in 1521 and colonizing the islands in 1565, Spain ruled for more than 300 years, bringing with them new political structures, Christianity, and Hispanic culture.

The late 19th century saw the growth of Filipino nationalism, which culminated in the 1896 Revolution and the country's declaration of independence in 1898. However, following the Spanish-American War, power quickly passed to the United States. Prior to Japan's occupation during World War II, the nation had developed democratic institutions, infrastructure, and public education under American rule.

The Philippines attained independence in 1946 following liberation in 1945.

Since then, the country has experienced periods of political hardship, such as the People Power Revolution of 1986 and the Marcos dictatorship, and it has continued to grow into a democratic nation with a diverse population.
